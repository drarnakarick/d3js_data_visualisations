Current work at Swinburne

My work at Swinburne is quite varied. I've worked on e-Research projects that focus on developing data management policy, and systems that promote and enable best practise in research. Initially I worked on the joint Swinburne/Australian National Data Service (ANDS) Metadata Stores Project and was responsible for overseeing the development and implementation of two MyTardis systems (for brain imaging and atom optics) and the Swin ReDBox research metadata store. I also worked on customisation and testing of the ReDBox system and the integration with Research Data Australia (RDA) and the National Library Archive (NLA). In conjunction with this project I wrote Swinburne's policies and guidelines concerning research data management and meeting compliance requirements set by the Australian Reserach Council (ARC). Over the past year or so my focus has shifted to addressing the current issues that impact data-intensive research and figuring out solutions to these problems. These include the difficulty in storing and analysing large datasets – the big data challenge, solving sustainability issues around large research infrastructure projects (e.g. NCRIS funded), up-skilling researchers to enable them to expoloit new data analysis tools and methods, providing alternative career paths in academia for data and software focussed reserchers, and providing training to enable scientists to transition into the tech-industry. In this respect I am somewhat biased towards astronomy and the issues arising from the next-generation of large-survey telescopes.

Swinburne Hacker Within

Swinburne Hacker Within is weekly, multi-disciplinary meetup for Swinburne PhD students and researchers. The goal of the project is to introduce reserchers to the plethora of open-source tools that can be used to increase productivity, enhance existing research projects, and demonstrate the value of social sciences, biosciences, engineering, astronomy, business and design. The Swinburne/ANDS Metadata Stores Project

In 2012, Swinburne was successful in obtaining funding from the Australian National Data Service (ANDS) for the development of MyTardis and ReDBox research data managment systems. The overall goal of the initiative was to implement a number of systems with the potental to support an university-wide solution for the discovery, sharing and re-use of rich data collections. The benefit of such systems would result in improved research data management compliance, increased visibility around Swinburne research data activities, a clear picture of research data needs for strategic planning, and increased data-discovery through high quality metadata.

MyTardis and MicroTardis

Data management systems for for brain imaging and atom optics

One component of ANDS Project involves the capture of brain imaging metadata, through the implementation of Tardis software linked to the Brain and Psychological Sciences Research Centre's (BPsyC) state of the art instrumentation. Customisation of the new BPsyC-Tardis system is a collaboration between Swinburne University and VeRSI, and addresses the specific needs of BpsyC researchers. The sensitive nature of the data and the legislation governing data retention often restricts data sharing in this field. Consequently the main driver for BPsyC-Tardis is the need for secure data storage, while still allowing collaborative sharing. Phase I development facilitates automatic capture, initial processing and storage of magnetoencephalographgy (MEG) non-invasive 'brain scanning' data, and the accompanying external metadata - for example audio and visual trigger information.

A similar system was implemented for the Centre for Quantum Optics and Science (CQOS - formerly CAOUS) Phase I development of CAOUS-Tardis facilitates the collection, initial processing and storage of data from the Renishaw In Via Raman Spectrometer and Olympus IX81 Confocal Microscope, and the their accompanying external metadata; for example information about cell and bacteria samples. Phase II development will include data ingestion from the Bruker Contour GT-K1 Optical Surface Profiles.
