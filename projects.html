At Swinburne

Overview

At Swinburne my work is quite varied, from working on e-Research projects that focus on developing data management policy, and systems that promote and enable best practise in research, to research training, to liasing with industry organisations such those established under NCRIS. For the Swinburne/ANDS Metadata Stores Project I was responsible for overseeing the development and implementation of two MyTardis systems (for brain imaging and atom optics) and the Swin ReDBox research metadata store. I also worked on the customisation and testing of the ReDBox system and the integration with Research Data Australia and the National Library Archive. As part of this work I developed the University's policies and guidelines concerning research data management and meeting compliance requirements set by the ARC and NHMRC. Over the past year my focus has shifted to addressing the current issues that impact data-intensive research and figuring out solutions to these problems. These include the difficulty in storing and analysing large datasets – the big data challenge, solving sustainability issues around large research infrastructure projects (e.g. NCRIS funded), up-skilling researchers to enable them to expoloit new data analysis tools and methods, providing alternative career paths in academia for data and software focussed reserchers, and providing training to enable scientists to transition into the tech-industry. In this respect I am somewhat biased towards astronomy research and the issues arising from the next-generation of large-survey telescopes, the value of mullti-discplinary data-driven discovery, and the benefit of universities taking the lead and establishing data-intensive research precincts.

    • Swinburne Hacker Within

    Swinburne Hacker Within is weekly, multi-disciplinary meetup for Swinburne PhD students and researchers. The goal of the project is to introduce reserchers – primarily from the s ocial sciences, biosciences, astronomy, and economics &ndahs; to the plethora of open-source tools that can be used to increase productivity and enhance existing projects, and to encourage the development of off-shoot projects and contribution to community-developed research tools. There are two reasons why this is important:

    1. To prepare researchers for alternative careers in the tech-industy. The rise of fellowship programs, for example Insight Data Fellows and Science to Data Science. Skilled data scientists are in great demand on the European and US job market and researchers with analytical backgrounds are being offered jobs in leading tech-companies.
    2. The tenets of scientific research (e.g., data control, reproducibility, and peer review) suffer in projects that fail to make use of current development tools such as version control, testing, and comphensive/automatic documentation. To avoid these pitfalls, the numerous Hacker Within Chapters exist for the purpose of sharing skills and best practices for computational scientific applications.

    The Swinburne/ANDS Metadata Stores Project

    In 2012, Swinburne was successful in obtaining funding from the Australian National Data Service (ANDS) for the development of MyTardis and ReDBox research data managment systems. The overall goal of the initiative was to implement a number of systems with the potental to support an university-wide solution for the discovery, sharing and re-use of rich data collections, with Swinburne data collections fed directly to Research Data Australia. Potential benefits of such systems inlcude improved research data management and compliance, increased visibility of Swinburne's research projects, data-discovery and the ability to assess needs and contribute to strategic planning decisions concering ITS infrastructure.

    MyTardis and MicroTardis

    One component of ANDS Project involves the capture of brain imaging metadata, through the implementation of Tardis software linked to the Brain and Psychological Sciences Research Centre's (BPsyC) state of the art instrumentation. Customisation of the new BPsyC-Tardis system is a collaboration between Swinburne University and VeRSI, and addresses the specific needs of BpsyC researchers. The sensitive nature of the data and the legislation governing data retention often restricts data sharing in this field. Consequently the main driver for BPsyC-Tardis is the need for secure data storage, while still allowing collaborative sharing. Phase I development facilitates automatic capture, initial processing and storage of magnetoencephalographgy (MEG) non-invasive 'brain scanning' data, and the accompanying external metadata - for example audio and visual trigger information.

    A similar system was implemented for the Centre for Quantum Optics and Science (CQOS - formerly CAOUS) Phase I development of CAOUS-Tardis facilitates the collection, initial processing and storage of data from the Renishaw In Via Raman Spectrometer and Olympus IX81 Confocal Microscope, and the their accompanying external metadata; for example information about cell and bacteria samples. Phase II development will include data ingestion from the Bruker Contour GT-K1 Optical Surface Profiles.
